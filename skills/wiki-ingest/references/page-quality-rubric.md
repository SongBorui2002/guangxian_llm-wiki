# Page Quality Rubric

Use this rubric to judge whether newly created wiki pages are actually useful.

## Source Pages

A good source page:

- says what the source covers
- says why the source matters
- points to the most important workflows, concepts, and entities that came from it
- exposes likely follow-up questions when the source is large

A weak source page:

- only summarizes the source at a high level
- provides no path to likely next questions

## Workflow Pages

A good workflow page:

- answers a real task question
- includes prerequisites, entry points, main steps, outputs, and common failures when available
- names relevant settings, windows, fields, or parameters when the source provides them
- cites the sections that support the steps

A weak workflow page:

- just paraphrases a chapter title
- says what the chapter is about but not how the task is done

## Concept Pages

A good concept page:

- defines the term in the source's working context
- explains why it matters
- attaches the term to real settings, fields, screens, constraints, or workflows when relevant
- distinguishes the term from adjacent concepts when confusion is likely

A weak concept page:

- gives only a broad dictionary-style explanation
- is not grounded in the actual source

## Domain Pages

A good domain page:

- acts as a navigation hub
- points to the important source, workflow, concept, and entity pages
- stays at the map level

A weak domain page:

- becomes the place where all detailed answers are dumped

## Final Test

Ask:

- Can a future query answer the likely question by reading this page?
- Or will the agent still need to reopen the raw source to get the real answer?

If the second answer is true too often, the page is under-compiled.
